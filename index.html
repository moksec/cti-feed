DKnife Linux toolkit hijacks router traffic to spy, deliver malware - bleepingcomputer.com
The DKnife Linux toolkit, attributed to a China-nexus threat actor, poses a significant and sophisticated threat by hijacking router traffic for espionage and malware delivery. Its long-term operation (since 2019, with C2s active as of January 2026) and advanced Adversary-in-the-Middle (AitM) capabilities enable deep packet inspection, credential harvesting, and extensive monitoring of user activities across various platforms. This indicates a targeted, state-sponsored effort to gather intelligence and maintain persistent access, representing a substantial risk to global organizations and individuals, particularly those operating with or within regions and services of interest to the threat actor.
Organizations should prioritize securing edge devices, including routers, through regular patching, strong authentication, and network segmentation. Implement robust network monitoring to detect anomalies such as DNS hijacking attempts or unusual internal traffic routing (e.g., to a 10.3.3.3 interface). Deploy Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) solutions across Linux, Windows, and Android systems to detect the presence of backdoors like ShadowPad or DarkNimbus. Utilize the provided Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) from Cisco Talos for proactive threat hunting and blocking. Educate end-users on phishing risks, and review security practices for software updates, especially for mobile applications and operating systems.
DKnife is an ELF framework comprised of seven Linux-based components, functioning as a post-compromise AitM toolkit on compromised edge devices (routers). Its modules include `dknife.bin`, `postapi.bin`, `sslmm.bin` (a HAProxy-derived reverse proxy), `yitiji.bin` (which creates a bridged TAP interface at 10.3.3.3 for traffic manipulation), `remote.bin` (an n2n VPN client), `mmdown.bin` (an Android APK downloader/updater), and `dkupdate.bin`. The toolkit is capable of delivering ShadowPad (for Windows, signed with a Chinese firm’s certificate) and DarkNimbus backdoors, with direct backdoor delivery to Android devices. Key functionalities encompass deep packet inspection, DNS hijacking, hijacking Android application updates and Windows binaries, POP3/IMAP credential harvesting, hosting phishing pages, disrupting anti-virus traffic, and detailed monitoring of user activity including messaging apps (WeChat, Signal), maps, news, calling, ride-hailing, and shopping. IoCs are available on the Cisco Talos GitHub repository. The threat actor is assessed with high confidence to be a China-nexus group.
